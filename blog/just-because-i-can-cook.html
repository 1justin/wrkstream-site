Just Because I Can Cook Doesn't Mean I Should Be a Chef

WRKSTREAM • 8 min read

I make a pretty solid risotto. Creamy, properly seasoned, the rice has that bite. My wife likes it. My kids tolerate it (they're 10 and 6, so that's actually high praise). I can hold my own at a dinner party.

But if you dropped me into the kitchen at Alinea, Chicago's legendary three-Michelin-star restaurant where Grant Achatz and his team are deconstructing food into something closer to performance art, I would be a liability. I wouldn't just be out of my depth. I'd be dangerous. I'd slow down everyone around me, I'd misuse the equipment, and the result would be something nobody should eat.

I can cook. I should not be a chef.

This distinction matters more right now than it has in decades, because the tools to create, build, and design are being handed to everyone. And the line between "I can do this" and "I should be doing this" is getting blurry in ways that are going to cost companies real money.

The Literacy Trap

AI tools, no-code platforms, and design software have democratized creation. That's genuinely great. A marketing manager can build a prototype in an afternoon. A CEO can generate a competitive analysis with a well-crafted prompt. A sales lead can mock up a dashboard without filing an IT ticket.

This is progress. People should be learning these tools. The more literate your team is in what's possible, the better the questions they'll ask, the smarter their requirements will be, and the more productively they'll work with the people who build things professionally.

But literacy is not expertise. And here's where things go sideways.

A CEO who learned to prompt an AI tool over the weekend starts thinking they don't need a product strategist. A marketing director who built a landing page in Squarespace figures they don't need a designer. A founder who shipped a prototype on Glide decides they don't need engineering for scale.

They can cook. They think they're chefs.

The Wine Test

I've studied wine casually for years. I can tell a Cab from a Pinot with reasonable accuracy. I know what "tannins" means without looking it up. I've done a few tastings. I can hold a conversation about wine at dinner without embarrassing myself.

I should not be your sommelier.

A sommelier doesn't just know wine. They understand how specific vintages interact with specific dishes. They read the table, the mood, the budget. They know which producers are consistent and which are coasting on reputation. They've tasted thousands of wines, not dozens. Their expertise isn't just knowledge. It's pattern recognition built on reps that I haven't done and probably never will.

That gap between "conversant" and "expert" is invisible to someone standing on the conversant side. You don't know what you don't know. And the Dunning-Kruger effect is undefeated.

The Design Version

This one hits close to home because I've spent 20 years in product development and I've watched it happen hundreds of times.

A stakeholder reviews a brand concept from a designer. Their feedback: "Can you make the logo bigger?"

That's the "I can cook" version of design feedback. It's not wrong, exactly. But it reveals that the person giving it doesn't have the vocabulary or the framework to say what they actually mean.

A literate stakeholder says something different. They might say: "I want this to feel more bold. Is there a way to increase the visual weight of the mark without scaling it up? Maybe through contrast, or simplifying what's around it?"

That's the difference. Not that you can do the designer's job. But that you understand enough about the discipline to collaborate with them effectively.

The Technology Version

Now bring this into the conversation every CEO in America is having right now: "What should we be doing about AI?"

"AI" has become an umbrella term that covers wildly different technologies, each with different applications, different limitations, and different price tags. When someone says "we need AI," that's about as useful as saying "we need food." Sure. But what kind? For what occasion? For how many people? Cooked how?

Here's a taste of what sits under that umbrella:

Large Language Models

These are what most people think of when they hear "AI." They generate text, analyze documents, draft emails, and power chatbots. They're incredibly useful and increasingly commoditized.

Machine Learning

The broader discipline of training models on data to make predictions. Fraud detection, demand forecasting, recommendation engines.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Connects a language model to your company's actual data, so it can answer questions grounded in your documents, your knowledge base, your internal systems.

Agentic AI

Where things are heading fast. These are systems that don't just respond to prompts. They take action. They browse, research, draft, schedule, execute multi-step workflows, and loop back for approval.

A CEO who understands these distinctions makes dramatically better decisions about where to invest, which vendor to trust, and what's realistic versus what's hype.

That's literacy. That's the risotto.

The chef is the person who knows which of these approaches solves your specific operational problem, how to architect them together, how to integrate them with your existing stack, and how to build it in a way that doesn't collapse the first time something unexpected happens.

Why This Matters for Your Business

Here's what I've seen go wrong, over and over:

A company hires a "prompt engineer" when they need a product strategist. They get clever prompts and no business architecture.

A founder builds a prototype with a no-code tool and assumes it's production-ready. It breaks at scale, and the rebuild costs more than doing it right the first time.

A leadership team buys an AI platform from a vendor who pitched great demos but doesn't understand their workflow. Six months later, nobody on the team uses it.

In every case, the problem wasn't the tool. It was the gap between knowing enough to be dangerous and knowing enough to be effective.

The fix isn't "don't learn to cook." The fix is: learn to cook AND know when to call the chef.
