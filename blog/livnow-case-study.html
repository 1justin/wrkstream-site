How We Built a $200K Platform for $20K, and What Every CEO Should Learn From It

WRKSTREAM • 8 min read

LivNow Relocation helps older adults and their families navigate one of life's most stressful transitions: moving into senior living communities. Their team coordinates everything from home sales to packing to settling into a new space. They are deeply good at what they do, with decades of corporate relocation expertise and the kind of genuine care for their clients that you can't train into people.

But like a lot of fast-growing companies with big ambitions and limited budgets, they had an operations problem that was outpacing their tools.

The Problem

LivNow's business runs on coordination. Every client move involves dozens of tasks across multiple service providers: realtors, movers, organizers, estate sale teams. The team was tracking all of it across spreadsheets, email threads, and memory.

They needed a single platform that could function as a CRM, project manager, and operational hub. A relocation management system that brought everything under one roof. When they went to market for a custom software solution, the quotes came back around $200,000. And what was being proposed was, at its core, a database with a limited user interface. A glorified spreadsheet with a login screen.

For a scrappy startup that hadn't raised significant capital, $200K wasn't a line item. It was a wall.

What We Built

WRKSTREAM came in with a different lens. Instead of hiring a traditional development shop, we used Glide (a no-code platform) to build the first version of the tool. Total cost: roughly 1/10th of what had been quoted.

The result was a polished, functional application that the LivNow team could use right away. It tracked clients, managed service provider relationships, coordinated task workflows, and gave the team visibility into every active relocation. This wasn't a band-aid or a prototype they were going to throw away. It was a real operational tool, built in weeks instead of months.

The team ran with it. They were energized, creative, and full of ideas for where to take it next.

The Evolution

As the product grew, we hit the natural ceiling of no-code tools. Glide is excellent for building fast, but when you need complex data relationships, custom logic, and advanced user permissions, you need more power under the hood.

We migrated the backend to Xano (a backend-as-a-service platform) and rebuilt the frontend on Weweb, giving us more flexibility and scalability without the cost of a fully custom codebase. The product matured from a no-code prototype into a real low-code application with serious operational capability.

This evolution, from no-code to low-code to a production-grade system, is a journey that more companies are going through now than ever before. And it's a journey that used to cost millions. We did it for a fraction of that.

What Made LivNow Special

I want to be clear about something: the LivNow team was exceptional to work with. They brought something to this engagement that money can't buy. Deep domain expertise. Relentless energy. A genuine talent for sales and team culture. They knew the relocation industry inside and out, had relationships with senior living communities across the country, and cared about their clients in a way that showed up in every conversation.

What we built together was more than an internal tool. It was a platform that could have been productized and sold to other relocation companies. That's how solid the foundation was.

The Universal Startup Lesson

Every startup hits the moment where ambition outpaces budget. It's almost a rite of passage. You build something that works, your team gets excited, and suddenly the roadmap doubles in size. Features multiply. Scope expands. And the budget that covered version one doesn't stretch to version five.

The companies that navigate this well are the ones who learn to treat software development as an ongoing relationship, not a one-time handoff. If you come from a physical product background, or from industries where you commission a thing and it gets delivered "finished," this is a hard adjustment. Software is never done. It's a living system that needs iteration, maintenance, and investment, and understanding that upfront changes everything about how you budget for it and how you work with the people who build it.

That mindset gap (between "deliver me a widget" and "let's build a system together") is the single most common source of friction between non-technical founders and their development partners. I've seen it in startups, in mid-market companies, and in enterprises. It is not unique to any one company. It's a pattern.

What This Taught Us

This engagement shaped how WRKSTREAM operates today. A few lessons that apply to any CEO thinking about technology:

Start with the smallest thing that works.

The Glide prototype proved the concept in weeks. If we'd started with a $200K custom build, the team would have waited months for something that might not have fit their real workflow. Build small, learn fast, then invest in what's proven.

No-code is a launchpad, not a destination.

No-code tools are incredible for proving ideas and getting to market fast. But they have ceilings. Plan for the evolution from the beginning. Know that if the product works, you'll outgrow the first version. And that's a good problem to have.

The real gap is translation.

LivNow didn't need a bigger engineering team. They needed someone who could take their deep operational knowledge and turn it into a product. That translation layer (understanding both the business problem and the technology landscape) is where most companies get stuck. It's also where WRKSTREAM lives.

Scope discipline is a survival skill.

The most exciting part of building software is that anything feels possible. The most dangerous part is exactly the same thing. Every feature that gets added without a clear business case is a tax on the entire system. The best products aren't the ones with the most features. They're the ones that do fewer things exceptionally well.

The 2026 Perspective

Here's what's changed since we built LivNow's platform: everything.

If we were starting this project today, we wouldn't use Glide. We wouldn't use Xano. We wouldn't use Weweb. We'd use AI-assisted development tools like Claude Code, paired with a backend-as-a-service platform like Supabase, and we'd produce something more powerful, more polished, and faster than what we built two years ago.

The cost curve for custom software has collapsed. What cost $200K from a dev shop in 2023 and $20K from us in 2024 can now be built for even less. Not because the work is less valuable, but because the tools have gotten that much more powerful. The expensive part is no longer the code. It's the product thinking, the design judgment, and the business context that shapes what gets built and why.

That's the shift every CEO needs to understand. You're not paying for lines of code anymore. You're paying for someone who knows what to build, how users will actually use it, and what "done" looks like for your specific business.

Who This Applies To

If you're a CEO or business owner sitting on a six-figure quote for custom software, pause. Ask yourself:

  1. Has anyone on your team actually used a no-code or AI-assisted tool to see what's possible before committing to custom development?
  2. Does the team quoting you understand your business, or are they just translating your requirements into code?
  3. Is the first version of this tool something that could be tested with real users in weeks instead of months?

If the answer to any of those is "no," you might be overpaying for the wrong thing.
