Why Mid-Market Companies Overpay for Software (And What CEOs Should Do Instead)

WRKSTREAM • 6 min read

Here's a pattern I've seen more times than I can count.

A company with $20M to $200M in revenue identifies an operational problem. Maybe it's tracking field operations. Maybe it's managing a sales pipeline that outgrew spreadsheets. Maybe it's coordinating logistics across multiple locations.

They go to a custom development shop. The shop scopes the project, presents a proposal, and quotes something between $150K and $500K. The leadership team doesn't have anyone with deep technical fluency, so they evaluate the proposal the only way they know how: does this vendor seem credible, and can we afford it?

Six to twelve months later, they have a tool that technically works but that nobody loves using. It solved the original problem but missed the actual workflow. It was built to spec but the spec was wrong because nobody on the dev team spent real time understanding the business.

And the $300K? About 75% of it went to engineering. Not to understanding the problem. Not to designing something people would actually adopt.

This is the pattern. And it's costing mid-market companies a fortune.

Why It Happens

Three forces converge to create this dynamic:

The vendor incentive is to overbuild.

Custom dev shops make money on hours and complexity. A simpler solution that takes 6 weeks doesn't pay the bills like a complex one that takes 6 months. This isn't malicious. It's structural.

The internal team doesn't know what's possible.

If you haven't been inside the modern development ecosystem, you don't know that tools like Supabase, Vercel, and AI-assisted development have collapsed the cost of building operational software by 90% or more.

Nobody in the room speaks both languages.

The business team knows the problem. The dev team knows the technology. But nobody is fluent in both.

The New Landscape

The cost of writing code has dropped dramatically. AI-assisted development tools can produce functional, production-grade software in days that would have taken months three years ago.

But here's what hasn't gotten cheaper: understanding your business well enough to know what to build. Designing something that fits your team's real workflow, not the idealized version on a whiteboard. Making sure the thing actually gets adopted.

Five Questions to Ask Before Signing a Six-Figure Software Proposal

  1. "Have you built something in our industry before?" Domain fluency matters more than technical credentials.
  2. "Can we see a working prototype in two weeks?" Modern tools make this possible. If a vendor needs three months before you see anything, the project is probably over-scoped.
  3. "What happens after launch?" Software is never done. Ask about ongoing support and iteration.
  4. "Who on your team will spend time understanding our business?" A few days with a business analyst isn't enough.
  5. "What's the simplest version of this that we could test with real users?" This reveals whether the vendor thinks in terms of learning or in terms of building.
