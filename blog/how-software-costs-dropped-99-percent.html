How Software Development Costs Dropped 99% in 3 Years (And What It Means for Your Business)

WRKSTREAM • 7 min read

In 2022, if you wanted a custom operational tool for your business, you had one option: hire a development shop. A team of engineers would spend months building your database, your API layer, your user interface, your authentication system, your hosting infrastructure. The bill landed somewhere between $150K and $500K depending on complexity.

Three years later, the same tool can be built in weeks for a fraction of that cost.

This isn't a prediction. It's already happening.

The Four Eras (So Far)

2022 to 2023: The Custom Dev Shop Era

The only game in town. You needed custom software, you hired an agency. The process was slow (3 to 12 months), expensive ($150K+), and heavily weighted toward engineering. Even a modest tool required a team of 5 to 10 people billing hourly for months.

2024: The No-Code Revolution

Platforms like Glide, Bubble, Softr, and Airtable changed the equation. The cost dropped from $150K to $10K to $30K. The speed went from months to weeks. The tradeoff was ceiling.

2025: The Low-Code + BaaS Bridge

Backend-as-a-service platforms like Supabase, Xano, and Appwrite gave builders the power of a custom backend without the cost. This was the bridge between "I can build a prototype" and "I can build a production system."

2026: AI-Assisted Development

Tools like Claude Code, Cursor, and Windsurf have collapsed the gap between "what I want to build" and "what I can build." A product thinker with business context can now direct AI to write production-grade code, build database schemas, create user interfaces, and deploy applications.

The cost of writing code has approached zero for many operational applications. But here's the part most people miss: the cost of code dropping doesn't mean your project costs less. It means your budget goes further.

The Real Shift: From Code Cost to Value Cost

For every $1 spent on building a product, companies spend $6 on services around it. Training, implementation, support, iteration, strategy.

When engineering consumed 75% of the budget, there was nothing left for the things that actually determine whether a tool gets adopted. Now that AI has compressed the engineering cost, that budget can be redistributed.

A company that would have spent $200K on pure code can now spend $30K on code and invest the rest in strategy, design, change management, and ongoing support. The total spend might be the same. The outcome is dramatically better.

What This Means If You Run a Business

If you're evaluating a software proposal right now:

Ask what percentage of the budget goes to engineering versus strategy, design, and post-launch support. If the answer is "mostly engineering," the proposal is structured for 2022, not 2026.

If you've been told "custom software is too expensive for us":

That was true three years ago. It probably isn't true anymore.

If you're already using AI tools internally:

Ask whether you're using them strategically or just tactically. There's a difference between "our team uses ChatGPT for emails" and "we've identified three operational workflows where AI can save 40 hours per week."

If you haven't started at all:

You're not as far behind as you think, but you're further behind than you'll be comfortable with in 12 months.

The Expertise Gap

As the cost of code drops, the value of knowing what to build goes up.

Anyone can prompt an AI tool to write code. Not everyone can look at a business operation, identify the highest-leverage problem, design a solution that fits the real workflow, and build it in a way that the team will actually adopt.

The technology has been democratized. The expertise hasn't.
